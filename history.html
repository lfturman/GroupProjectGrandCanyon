History of The Grand Canyon

General Facts and Figures

  • The Grand Canyon is approximately 277 miles (446 km) long.
  • It reaches a depth of over 6,000 feet (1,829 meters).
  • The width of the canyon varies from 4 to 18 miles (6.4 to 29 km).
  • It exposes nearly 2 billion years of Earth's geological history.
  • The Colorado River continues to shape the canyon today.
  • Over 5 million visitors explore the Grand Canyon annually.
  • The park covers an area of about 1.2 million acres (490,000 hectares).
  • It is home to more than 1,500 plant species and 600 animal species.
  • The Grand Canyon Skywalk offers a unique glass bridge experience.
  • It was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1979.

Geological Formation

The Grand Canyon is a geological marvel, carved by the mighty Colorado River over 5 to 6 million years. The canyon walls expose layers of rock that trace nearly two billion years of Earth's history, offering geologists a unique cross-section of the Earth's crust.

Native American Presence

Humans have inhabited the region for more than 12,000 years, with archaeological evidence including spear points from Paleo-Indians and intricate split-twig figurines found in canyon caves. Numerous Native American tribes have historic and cultural ties to the canyon, including:

  • Ancestral Puebloans: Flourished in the area, developing agriculture and building stone dwellings (pueblos) before leaving due to a prolonged drought in the 13th century.
  • Havasupai: Known as the "people of the blue-green waters," they have lived in the canyon for at least 800 years and continue to reside in the village of Supai.
  • Navajo, Hualapai, and Southern Paiute: These tribes also have deep historical connections and communities along the canyon's edges.

European Exploration and Park Status

The first Europeans to reach the Grand Canyon were Spanish explorers in the 1540s, guided by Hopi people. However, it wasn't until U.S. Army Major John Wesley Powell led the first successful river expedition through the canyon in 1869 that the area gained significant scientific and national attention.

Early settlers initially sought mineral resources like copper, but tourism soon proved more profitable.

  • 1893: President Benjamin Harrison first protected the canyon as a forest reserve.
  • 1908: President Theodore Roosevelt designated it a national monument to preserve its grandeur.
  • 1919: The Grand Canyon was officially established as a National Park by an Act of Congress signed by President Woodrow Wilson.
